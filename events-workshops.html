Workshops

Have you ever looked up at a surveillance monitor while standing in line at the bank and wondered if anyone was looking at you? Have you ever noticed a CCTV sign in the window of a store and wondered whether they are used for collecting information about your shopping habits as well as making sure you’re not a shoplifter? Ever watch a piece of CCTV crime footage on the news and feel more sure they’d catch the bad guy because they had him (or her) on film?

We are researchers at the University of Toronto involved with a project about how people think about and experience video surveillance technologies (i.e. CCTV cameras) in everyday life, funded by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada.

Previous Workshops

If you’ve ever thought about video surveillance, or even if you haven’t really thought about it much but think it might be interesting to learn some more about, we’d like to invite you to participate in a workshop at the University of Toronto Library on April 17th in room 307 of the Bissel Building (attached to the north wing of the library). Video surveillance is increasingly being used in public and private spaces, and new technologies are changing the ways that visual information can be used. It’s important that the regulations that tell organisations how these technologies can and can’t be used reflect the values of citizens, and your opinions can help us figure out the things that Canadians like or don’t like about video surveillance.

Who is Watching You? Video Surveillance Workshop - Apr 17, 2013

Wednesday, April 17th from 5:00 PM until 9:00 PM
Location: 140 St. George Street (Bissell Building), Room #307
